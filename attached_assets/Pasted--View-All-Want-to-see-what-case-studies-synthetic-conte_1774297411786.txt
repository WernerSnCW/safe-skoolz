For primary-age pupils, I’d anchor everything around simple “feelings-first” journeys, cartoon-style placeholders, and very constrained language, while using parents/teachers/PTA case studies to show the heavier functionality.

Below are concrete case study ideas, synthetic content patterns, and a few feature tweaks tailored to under‑11s.

1. Primary-friendly pupil journeys
A. “Something unkind happened” (incident reporting)

Use a single recurring primary character (e.g. “Luna” / “Max”) and 2–3 classmates.

Scenario titles:

“Someone keeps leaving me out at playtime”

“A boy in my class pushes me in the line”

“Kids in my group made a mean WhatsApp name”

Language on-screen:

“What is happening?” → tiles: “Someone is unkind to me”, “Someone is unkind to another child”, “I saw something unkind”.

Incident types simplified labels on top of your real taxonomy:

Bullying → “Being unkind on purpose”

Physical → “Hitting, kicking or pushing”

Verbal → “Mean or rude words”

Relational → “Leaving people out on purpose”

Cyberbullying → “Unkind messages or pictures online”

Emotional-state picker:

5 faces: “Happy”, “OK”, “Worried”, “Sad”, “Very upset”.

Short helper text: “Choose the face that feels most like you today.”

Placeholders:

Name fields prefilled with “Luna in Year 3”, “Sam in Class 4B”.

Description examples:

“Three boys keep calling me names at lunch time.”

“My friend is being pushed in football and told to go away.”

Use teachers’ view placeholders to show the same case classified correctly, severity set, and moved through the workflow.

2. Diary examples and “AI scanner” narrative
Make the diary feel like a safe emotional outlet, and show the AI as a “worry helper”, not a watcher.

Normal diary entries:

“Today we did painting. I felt proud of my picture.”

“I was a bit nervous about reading aloud but it went okay.”

Low-level concern:

“Kids at football always laugh when I miss. I don’t want to go anymore.”

High-concern (for demo only, no graphic detail):

“I feel scared to go home. I don’t sleep well and cry most nights.”

How you visually explain the scanner:

Simple info bubble for kids: “If your diary shows big worries, a trusted adult may get a secret ‘please check on me’ message. They won’t see your diary page.”

For staff demo: show a pattern_alert record with a fixed message like: “Diary scanner has noticed signs that [Pupil A] may need extra support. Please check in with this pupil.”

This makes the AI function legible to parents and regulators while remaining non-intrusive for children.

3. Diagnostics: primary pupil surveys
Keep wording ultra-simple and concrete, using emoji faces.

Question stems per category:

Awareness & Prevalence: “If someone is unkind a lot, do adults in school find out?”

Trust & Reporting: “If you tell an adult here a worry, do they listen and help?”

Culture & Wellbeing: “Do you feel safe in school most days?”

Safeguarding Knowledge: “Do you know which adult to talk to if something scares you?”

Emoji Likert:

Faces from very sad → very happy, with words: “Never”, “Sometimes”, “Lots of times”, “Always”.

Placeholders:

Sample radar chart for “Year 3–4 pupils” with one weak axis (e.g. Trust & Reporting), then an auto‑generated action plan showing 2–3 concrete actions:

“More ‘who can I talk to?’ posters in classrooms.”

“Circle time once a week about kindness and worries.”

4. Behaviour and “kindness-first” framing
Use behaviour tracking to reinforce pro‑social behaviour, not just compliance.

Point events:

“Helped a friend who was sad” → Kindness +2.

“Included someone new in our game” → Teamwork +2.

“Stayed calm when something went wrong” → Respect +1.

Pupil-facing UI:

“You reached Silver for kindness!”

Simple progress bars per category (“You are close to Gold in Effort”).

Class view:

Leaderboard NOT by individuals only, but also “Class 3A kindness total this week”.

This links directly to anti‑bullying culture and Convivèxit “convivencia” language without exposing real pupils.

Artefacts (mocked):

B. Teacher / Head of Year

Teacher logs restorative actions, checks behaviour summary, adds a note to SENCO caseload if patterns persist.

Parent story:

“Parent sees two low‑grade incident reports and a dip in behaviour points; uses messaging to contact the DSL, not the WhatsApp class group.”

PTA story:

PTA uses diagnostic results dashboard to flag that Year 5 pupils report low trust in adults, raises concern via PTA portal, co‑designs new parent workshop and playground charter.

These case studies show how the system supports whole‑school culture, not just incident firefighting.
