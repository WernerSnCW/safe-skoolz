Case study 1 – “Child A creates new bullies”
Narrative (human story)

Child A has a history of low‑level bullying.

Children B and C previously had no incident history.

Over a term, A, B, and C repeatedly target Child D (no prior incidents).

In interviews, B and C say they joined in because they are scared of A and “don’t want to be next”.

What safeskoolz would show

Pattern rules:

same_victim_3_incidents fires for Child D.

group_targeting fires for the trio A+B+C against D.

repeat_perpetrator fires for A as a frequent instigator.

Timeline view:

Incidents show D as victim with A always present, B and C joining later.

Emotional data:

D’s emotional_state_before/after scores drop over time.

A’s behaviour_points show incidents across different classmates.

Intervention enabled

Coordinator sees A as the origin, B and C as coerced followers, D as primary victim.

School runs:

Restorative work and clear sanctions for A.

Protective work with B and C about peer pressure and fear.

Safety plan and check‑ins for D.

PTA/parents see, via anonymised PTA report, that the school is tackling “ring‑leader plus recruited bullies” patterns, not just isolated incidents.

Case study 2 – “Retaliation kills reporting”
Narrative

Child X reports being bullied by Y and Z.

School intervenes weakly; word gets out.

Y and Z start mocking X as “snitch” in corridors.

Child E bullies Child F for staying friends with X.

Children who report start being targeted; reporting drops sharply because kids are scared of retaliation.

What safeskoolz would show

Incident patterns:

Initial incidents: X victim, Y+Z perpetrators.

Follow‑on incidents: X and F victim, E/Y/Z perpetrators, category “bullying”/“retaliation”.

Trend:

After first intervention, total incidents don’t drop, but new victims appear linked to X.

Pupil diagnostic survey shows:

Lower “Trust & Reporting” scores for pupils (“If I report, it will make things worse”).

Pattern rules:

group_targeting on X,

repeat_perpetrator on Y and Z,

Potential AI‑flagged messages about “snitch” language.

Intervention enabled

Coordinator sees a retaliation cluster, not “problem solved”.

Interventions:

Explicit “anti‑retaliation” policy communication in assemblies and to parents.

Stronger sanctions for Y/Z/E focused on retaliatory behaviour.

Private reassurance to pupils that retaliation will be treated as a separate serious offence.

PTA dashboard shows:

Short‑term spike in incidents but improved follow‑up and then a later recovery in survey trust scores as culture changes.

Case study 3 – “Child Q’s material bullying”
Narrative

Child Q regularly mocks classmates about clothes, phones, brands, and holidays.

Individual events seem minor (“just teasing”), so they are not joined up mentally by staff.

Several children start avoiding Q and feel “poor” or “left out”.

What safeskoolz would show

Incident tags:

Multiple incidents for Q as perpetrator with category “bullying” + subtag “discrimination/material” or “socio‑economic”.

Pattern rules:

repeat_perpetrator fires for Q.

Analytics:

Coordinator dashboard: cluster of incidents with similar free‑text keywords (“clothes”, “phone”, “cheap”, “holiday”).

Diagnostic survey: pupils in Q’s class rate “I feel respected at school” and “People don’t judge me for what I have” significantly lower than average.

Intervention enabled

School identifies a theme: bullying around material status.

Interventions:

Work directly with Q on empathy and respect.

Class‑wide PSHE units on money, equality, and respect.

Review reward systems that may glorify visible wealth markers.

PTA sees in the annual report:

“We identified a pattern of material‑based bullying in one class and implemented X, Y, Z responses.”

Case study 4 – “Classroom volatility: P and T together”
Narrative

Child P and Child T are fine separately but highly disruptive together.

Teachers experience “bad days” but don’t tie it to P+T as a pair.

What safeskoolz would show

Behaviour_points and incidents:

spikes in points and low behaviour ratings on days/lessons where P and T share a group or table.

Analytics:

Heatmap view of incidents by class group and seating plan (if captured).

Repeated co‑occurrence of P and T in incident records.

Possible pattern rule:

Custom rule same_pair_escalating showing that when P and T are together, severity tends to rise.

Intervention enabled

Simple but powerful action: separate P and T in timetabling/seating.

Monitor post‑change:

Incidents and behaviour points in that class drop.

This becomes a micro‑case study of how small structural adjustments, guided by data, reduce low‑level disruption and free staff time.

How to package these for Morna
For each case, prepare three slides or sections:

“What was happening” – 3–4 bullet story about the children.

“What safeskoolz surfaced” – screenshot‑style descriptions of pattern alerts, dashboards, and survey results.

“What the school did differently” – concrete interventions + how you’d evidence improvement (fewer incidents, better survey scores, fewer pattern alerts).

This gives you:

Child‑level empathy (parents and teachers feel the reality).

Data‑level clarity (why a system is needed).

Intervention‑level credibility (this is not just logging; it changes decisions).

Case study – “Slow collapse: Child M”
Narrative (human story)

Child M starts the year as a settled pupil with no incident history.

Over several weeks, small things happen: eye‑rolling, excluding from games, whispers about M’s appearance and work.

M doesn’t initially report; they just “feel bad about school”.

At home, parents notice M is quieter, more reluctant to go to school, but can’t point to a single big event.

What safeskoolz would show

Pupil diary data

Early weeks: mood entries mostly 4–5.

Over 3–4 weeks: mood drops to 2–3 with notes like “no one wanted me on their team”, “they laughed when I spoke”.

mood_decline rule fires: average mood ≤2 over 5+ entries in 14 days → amber alert to SENCO + coordinator.
​

Incident / behaviour signals

1–2 “low‑level” incidents logged by a teacher (e.g. “teasing”, “exclusion at break”) with M as victim but category set to “minor”.

No one incident hits “red” on its own, but combined with diary data the system shows a pattern of emotional distress.
​

Diagnostics and pattern alert

Diagnostic survey: M’s answers show low scores on “I feel I belong” and “I feel safe telling someone if something is wrong”.

Pattern engine links:

repeated low mood,

minor bullying incidents,

low trust in reporting.

Coordinator dashboard highlights M under an “at‑risk of withdrawal” tile.

Intervention enabled

SENCO and coordinator proactively invite M for a check‑in (“We’ve noticed school seems harder lately, can we talk?”), rather than waiting for a crisis.

They:

Explore what’s been happening socially and in class.

Identify a small group who have been quietly excluding or mocking M.

Put in place:

a named safe adult M can message,

targeted work with the peers involved,

small protective arrangements (e.g. buddy at break, seating that reduces exposure).

Over the next month, safeskoolz shows:

Mood gradually rising back toward 3–4.

No new bullying incidents involving M.

Follow‑up diagnostic (or next term’s survey) showing improved “belonging” and “trust” scores for that class.

This case study is powerful because it demonstrates that safeskoolz catches slow emotional collapse that would otherwise be invisible until the child refuses school, self‑harms, or explodes.
